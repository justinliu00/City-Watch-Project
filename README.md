# City-Watch-Project
## About City Watch

City Watch is an AI-powered urban hazard reporting platform designed to improve the way residents document and communicate public infrastructure and safety concerns. Urban hazards such as potholes, flooding, fallen trees, damaged streetlights, blocked sidewalks, overflowing garbage, and exposed infrastructure can remain unreported until they become more disruptive or dangerous. Even when residents recognize a problem, they may not know how serious it is, which department should respond, or how to describe the issue in a clear and actionable manner. City Watch addresses this challenge by transforming a resident-submitted photograph into a structured and professional hazard assessment for authorities to utilize.

The application guides users through a simple reporting process. A resident uploads a photograph of the hazard, optionally provides a written description, enters the location, and states when the issue began. City Watch then prepares the image for analysis by converting it to a standard color format, resizing it to a maximum resolution of 640 by 640 pixels, and compressing it to reduce processing time. The optimized image and resident-provided information are sent to a locally hosted Qwen2.5-VL vision-language model through Ollama.

The artificial intelligence model analyzes the photograph and returns a structured response. This response identifies the most likely visible issue, assigns a danger level, calculates a priority score, estimates the model’s confidence, and explains the visual evidence used to support the assessment. The model also provides a safety recommendation for nearby residents, identifies the city authority most likely responsible for the problem, generates a concise service report, and explains the reasoning behind the selected danger level and priority score.

City Watch is designed to produce actionable information rather than a general image description. The application organizes the AI-generated analysis into a clear results page that allows users to quickly understand the nature and urgency of the hazard. It also creates a structured service report containing the date and time of generation, reported location, reported start time, issue classification, danger level, priority score, confidence level, responsible authority, incident summary, visible evidence, recommended city response, and resident safety guidance. The completed report can be downloaded as a text file for future reference or potential submission to a municipal department.

The platform is built with Python and Streamlit, while Pillow is used for image preparation and compression. Ollama provides the local model-serving environment, and the `qwen2.5vl:3b` model performs the visual analysis. The interface also incorporates custom HTML, CSS, and JavaScript to create a more polished and interactive user experience. D3.js, TopoJSON, and World Atlas data are used to render a scroll-responsive animated globe that rotates toward an approximate location determined through IP-based geolocation. The application includes responsive form elements, automatic scrolling between reporting steps, image previews, loading indicators, status notifications, and a custom dark visual design.

A major design goal of City Watch is accessibility. Residents should not need technical knowledge of city infrastructure or municipal department structures to create an effective report. The system helps translate a simple photograph and brief description into language that is more organized, specific, and useful for city personnel. Another objective is prioritization. By assigning a danger level and numerical priority score, City Watch demonstrates how artificial intelligence could help municipalities review large numbers of reports and identify situations that may require faster attention.

City Watch also emphasizes responsible use of artificial intelligence. The model is instructed to rely primarily on visible evidence from the photograph and to use the resident’s written description only as supporting context. It is specifically directed not to invent injuries, fires, leaks, or infrastructure damage that cannot be observed. When the image is unclear, the model is expected to lower its confidence score. The system also avoids advising residents to touch, move, or repair dangerous infrastructure. These safeguards are intended to reduce unsupported conclusions and encourage safer recommendations.

The current version of City Watch is a hackathon prototype and is not connected to an official municipal reporting system. It does not automatically submit reports to a city department, dispatch emergency personnel, or guarantee that the recommended authority matches the exact organizational structure of every municipality. The accuracy of the analysis may vary depending on image quality, lighting, camera angle, visibility of the hazard, resident-provided context, and the limitations of the local vision-language model. For these reasons, the generated assessment should be treated as decision-support information rather than an authoritative public-safety determination.

City Watch is not an emergency service. Residents should move away from any immediate danger and contact the appropriate emergency services when a situation presents a threat to life or involves severe flooding, fire, exposed electrical equipment, a suspected gas leak, or another urgent hazard. Every AI-generated report should be reviewed by an authorized city employee before official action is taken.

The long-term vision for City Watch is to create a more efficient connection between residents and local governments. Future development could include direct integration with municipal 311 systems, automatic coordinate collection, interactive hazard mapping, duplicate-report detection, report status tracking, community verification, multilingual support, persistent report history, infrastructure analytics, and a dedicated dashboard for city employees. By combining computer vision, structured reporting, risk prioritization, and a resident-focused interface, City Watch demonstrates how locally hosted artificial intelligence could help communities identify, communicate, and respond to urban hazards more efficiently.
